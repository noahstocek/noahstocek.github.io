Past and Ongoing Research

Master's at University of Western Ontario.

My Master's work involved extending the photogenerated charge carrier by linearly increasing voltage (photo-CELIV) mobility measurement technique in order to spatially map the photogenerated charge mobility throughout the cross-section of a solar cell. This provides insight into the roll of each layer in the device, and can help with optimization in the designing of new solar cell devices. Summer 2019

Research assistant at University of Western Ontario.

Worked on developping the Charge Extraction by Linearly Increasing Voltage (CELIV) experiment. This is a very powerful and easy technique for studying the internal properties of semiconductors, specifically the charge carrier mobility. My interest with this experiment is specifically to study the mobility of photo-induced charge carrier mobility of solar cells. This is done by pulsing a laser onto solar cell, and then applying a linear voltage to pull the newly created free charges out through the electrodes.

September 2018-April 2019

Undergraduate honours project in UNB's MRI lab. Supervised by Dr. Ben Newling.

Project involved measuring bulk flow propagators of flowing sample (water) using a modification of the well documented DIFFTRAIN pulse sequence approach. In this work we kept our pulse durations short (on the order of hundreds of microseconds), equal to our recycle time, and constant throughout the measurement. To do this, a small tip angle (9.5 degrees) was used. We also reorganized the bipolar gradient switches from how they are traditionally applied in order to minimize the amount of work that our gradient coils must do, which will in turn help us ensure we are measuring the centre of k-space on each bipolar gradient switch. One bit of new physics that we are hoping to uncover with this work is a sense of the time development of bulk flow propagators. By increasing the time of our pulse durations we will be able to see this, and are expecting our propagators to change with these pulse durations. Summer 2018

Research assistant in UNB's MRI lab. Supervised by Dr. Ben Newling.

Work included writing code in visual basic on TNMR software to automate calculation of a timing parameter that we have called Time Shift Trim (TST). Due to the unavoidable self inductance in gradient coils, they tend to slightly resist being switched to apply a new value (a necessary function in all MRI experiments). Due to this, our motion encoding gradient does not cross 0 at the exact time that we think it does, and thus we end up inadvertantly applying our radio frequency pulse at the wrong time, and effectively don't measure the centre of k-space. The centre of k-space contains the largest signal of our entire conjugate space and by missing this we lose valuable information about our system. By adding this adjustable TST parameter before our RF pulse we can delay it so that we apply it at the exact right time and measure the things we mean to measure. The one draw back is that the TST parameter can be very tedious and time consuming to determine, and depends on many specific parameters of the experiment being done. Therefore I developped a program to determine and set this time parameter quickly and efficiently, making this process much easier for the experimenter. I then helped with diffusion tensor imaging experiments on turbulent systems in an effort to measure the time scales on which turbulent flow is anisotropic.
