Past and Ongoing Research

September 2018-April 2019

Undergraduate honours project in UNB's MRI lab. Supervised by Dr. Ben Newling.

Project involves measuring bulk flow propagators of flowing sample (water doped with copper sulfate) using the well documented DIFFTRAIN pulse sequence approach. In this work we are keeping our pulse durations short (on the order of hundreds of microseconds) and constant throughout the measurement. This short time parameter technique has been proven in other settings to minimize imaging artefacts associated with the time development of NMR signals, and we are expecting to obtain similar results for the measurement of bulk flow propagators. We are also planning on reorganizing the bipolar gradient switches from how they are traditionally applied in order to minimize the amount of work that our gradient coils must do, which will in turn help us ensure we are measuring the centre of k-space on each bipolar gradient switch. One bit of new physics that we are hoping to uncover with this work is a sense of the time development of bulk flow propagators. By increasing the time of our pulse durations we will be able to see this, and are expecting our propagators to change with these pulse durations.

Summer 2018

Research assistant in UNB's MRI. Supervised by Dr. Ben Newling.

Work included writing code in visual basic on TNMR software to automate calculation of a timing parameter that we have called Time Shift Trim (TST). Due to the unavoidable self inductance in gradient coils, they tend to slightly resist being switched to apply a new value (a necessary function in all MRI experiments). Due to this, our motion encoding gradient does not cross 0 at the exact time that we think it does, and thus we end up inadvertantly applying our radio frequency pulse at the wrong time, and effectively don't measure the centre of k-space. The centre of k-space contains the largest signal of our entire conjugate space and by missing this we lose valuable information about our system. By adding this adjustable TST parameter before our RF pulse we can delay it so that we apply it at the exact right time and measure the things we mean to measure. The one draw back is that the TST parameter can be very tedious and time consuming to determine, and depends on many specific parameters of the experiment being done. Therefore I developped a program to determine and set this time parameter quickly and efficiently, making this process much easier for the experimenter. I then helped with diffusion tensor imaging experiments on turbulent systems in an effort to measure the time scales on which turbulent flow is anisotropic.

Summer 2016

Research assistant in Biomedical Engineering lab at Dalhousie University.

Work involved mixing bone glue samples made of various polymers in an attempt to find a mixture with the right properties that could be successfully inserted in a broken bone to help speed up healing. In order to do this, I helped assemble equipment to melt and mix different polymers into a sample. These would then be spread on bone samples which would be glued together. Once dried we would test the strength and hydrophobicity of the glue.
